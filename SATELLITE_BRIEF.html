How Elon Musk's Starlink works, what it costs, and what satellites power global video broadcasting.

Section 01

What Is Starlink?

SpaceX's global internet network built from thousands of small satellites in low Earth orbit.

The core idea: Traditional internet satellites sit 35,000 km away — so far that signals take 600ms to travel. Starlink satellites orbit at just 550 km. That proximity slashes latency to ~25ms, making it fast enough for video calls, gaming, and real-time applications — from anywhere on Earth.

🛰️ Satellite Type

  • LEO (Low Earth Orbit) — 550 km altitude
  • Each satellite weighs ~300 kg (v1.5) or ~800 kg (v2 Mini)
  • Flat-panel design with deployable solar array
  • Ka/Ku-band radio + laser inter-satellite links
  • Autonomous collision-avoidance (AI-driven)
  • Designed to de-orbit and burn up after ~5 years

📡 Ground Equipment

  • Flat phased-array dish ("Dishy") — no manual pointing needed
  • Electronically steers beam to track satellites as they pass
  • Connects to nearest satellite overhead every ~90 seconds
  • Built-in WiFi router included
  • Power: ~50–75W during operation
  • Works in rain, snow, moderate wind

🔗 Laser Links (Space Lasers)

  • v2 satellites have inter-satellite laser links
  • Data travels between satellites in space — not via ground
  • Faster than fibre for long-distance routes (light travels faster in vacuum)
  • Enables coverage over oceans and polar regions
  • Key for military / remote use cases
Section 02

How Starlink Works — Step by Step

From your dish to the internet in under 25 milliseconds.

1
Your dish sends a signal up

The flat phased-array antenna on your roof electronically locks onto the nearest Starlink satellite passing overhead. No mechanical pointing — the beam steers itself using thousands of tiny antenna elements.

2
Satellite receives and routes

The satellite receives your request on Ku-band radio. It either relays it down to the nearest SpaceX ground station, or — on laser-linked satellites — passes it across the constellation via space lasers to reach a ground station closer to your destination.

3
Ground station connects to internet

SpaceX ground stations (called "gateways") connect the satellite network to the terrestrial internet backbone. There are hundreds of gateways globally to minimise the final hop distance.

4
Response travels back

The return path is the same in reverse — internet → gateway → satellite → your dish. The whole round trip takes ~25ms. Compare that to 600ms for a traditional geostationary satellite.

5
Seamless handoff between satellites

Because Starlink satellites move fast across the sky, your dish hands off between satellites every ~90 seconds automatically — similar to how your phone switches cell towers. You never notice it.

Section 04

What Type of Satellite Powers Video Broadcasting?

TV and video broadcasting uses a completely different type of satellite to Starlink — here's why.

📺 GEO Satellites — The TV Broadcasting Standard

Geostationary Earth Orbit (GEO) satellites sit at 35,786 km altitude directly above the equator. At this height they orbit at exactly the same speed as Earth's rotation — so they appear completely stationary in the sky. One satellite covers 1/3 of the Earth's surface continuously.

  • Used by: Sky, DirecTV, Eutelsat, SES, Intelsat, Arabsat, Nilesat
  • One satellite covers entire continents — a single dish can receive it forever without moving
  • Broadcasts the same signal to millions of dishes simultaneously (one-to-many)
  • Ku-band (12–18 GHz) and Ka-band (26–40 GHz) for TV; C-band for older systems
  • High-definition and 4K video require ~20–100 Mbps per channel — GEO handles this easily
  • Latency is 600ms+ — fine for broadcast TV, not good for internet or real-time use
  • Cost per satellite: $150M–$400M · Lifespan: 15–20 years

🛰️ LEO Satellites — Emerging Video Delivery (OTT / Streaming)

Newer video delivery over the internet (Netflix, YouTube, live sports streaming) increasingly uses LEO constellations like Starlink. Not traditional broadcast — instead streaming unicast to individual users.

  • One connection per dish — personalised streams, not broadcast
  • Low latency enables live sports streaming without delay
  • Used in locations with no fibre: remote resorts, ships, aircraft in-flight entertainment
  • Growing use: airlines (JSX, Hawaiian Airlines), cruise ships, superyachts

📡 Key GEO Operators

  • SES — 70+ GEO satellites, global TV + data
  • Eutelsat — Europe, Middle East, Africa TV
  • Intelsat — global backbone, news networks
  • Arabsat — Arab world TV broadcasting
  • Nilesat — North Africa, Middle East
  • DirecTV / Sky — consumer TV delivery

🔑 Quick Rule of Thumb

  • Broadcasting to millions at once? → GEO satellite
  • Interactive internet / two-way data? → LEO (Starlink)
  • Remote location with no fibre? → LEO for speed, GEO for backup
  • Watching live TV on a dish? → GEO, always
  • Streaming Netflix on a ship? → LEO (Starlink Maritime)
